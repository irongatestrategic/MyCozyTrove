## Who This Is For

**Choose the Meedo Apollo A86 M if:** You move camp every few days rather than daily, you're entering RTT camping without committing to premium pricing, and a 3–5 minute setup routine isn't a friction point. The 130 lb weight is easier on vehicles with tighter dynamic load limits.

**Choose the Meedo Zeus I M if:** You break camp every morning, drive forest service roads with low-hanging debris, or arrive at sites after dark in bad weather. The 60-second deployment justifies the $200 premium when you're doing it repeatedly.

**Neither is right if:** You camp primarily at developed sites with level pads, your vehicle roof rack is factory crossbars (see load rating section below), or you're not prepared for the weight penalty either option adds to fuel economy on long hauls.

### What It Delivers

The Apollo A86 M is a clamshell soft shell with a 3000MM waterproof-rated fabric body and a 4-season construction. The telescoping ladder adjusts for different vehicle heights.

The 3000MM rating holds up under sustained rain when seams are properly sealed. This is not a fair-weather shelter; it's a legitimate 4-season option at a soft shell price point.

### Pros

- Lower upfront cost (~$1,100 vs. ~$1,300)
- ~130 lbs — 20 lbs lighter than the Zeus, relevant if your rack's dynamic rating is marginal
- 50.4 sq ft floor space fits two people without significant compromise
- 3000MM waterproof fabric rated for 4-season use
- Fabric cover allows more compression for stuffing in extra bedding when closed

### Cons

- 3–5 minute setup and takedown — a real time cost across a week of daily moves
- PVC cover degrades faster than rigid shells under UV exposure without regular treatment
- Fabric sides generate audible noise in sustained high winds
- Less aerodynamic profile increases drag on highway driving compared to a hard shell
- Setup in high wind is harder — fabric catches air during deployment

### Real Use Case

An angler running a 7-day trip across three public land units, setting up camp every other night. With the Apollo at ~130 lbs on the rack, two occupants at 175 lbs each, and 20 lbs of bedding, the static rack load is approximately 500 lbs. The 3–5 minute setup is manageable on a two-day rotation. The $1,100 price point keeps the overall trip budget lower without sacrificing elevated shelter and 4-season coverage.

## Meedo Zeus I M: Hard Shell

### What It Delivers

The Zeus I M uses a rigid ABS or aluminum outer shell. It deploys in roughly 60 seconds via a clamshell or pop-top mechanism. The shell protects tent fabric and bedding from branches, hail, and trail debris during transit — material that will eventually compromise a PVC cover over repeated contact. The 3000MM waterproof rating matches the Apollo; both tents share 4-season fabric and a 4.5-inch foam mattress.

The streamlined shell reduces wind noise while driving and creates less aerodynamic drag than a soft shell at highway speeds. The rigid top also allows mounting accessories — solar panels or recovery gear — where the structure supports the load.

### Pros

- 60-second deployment, including ladder extension
- Rigid shell resists abrasion from branches and debris on forest service roads
- Lower wind noise at highway speeds; less drag than soft shell profile
- Quieter interior in high winds — no fabric flapping
- Maintains appearance and structural integrity longer under repeated UV and weather exposure

### Cons

- ~$1,300 — $200 more than the Apollo
- ~150 lbs — 20 lbs heavier, a factor for vehicles near their rack's dynamic load limit
- Bedding storage when closed is more constrained — the rigid shell limits compression packing
- Hard shell damage (cracks, significant dents) requires more involved repair than patching PVC fabric
- Fixed external dimensions can be a clearance issue in low garages or on tight single-track

### Real Use Case

A solo camper on a 30-day expedition hitting remote fishing spots and hiking trailheads daily. Breaking camp every morning means 30 setups and 30 takedowns. At 60 seconds per cycle versus 3–5 minutes, the Zeus saves roughly 60–120 minutes of setup labor across the trip — plus eliminates the frustration of wrestling a fabric tent in wind or rain. At ~150 lbs, the rack load calculation with one occupant (175 lbs) and 20 lbs of gear sits around 345 lbs static — well within a properly rated aftermarket rack's capacity.

## Rack Load Rating: The Calculation Most Buyers Skip

Before purchasing either tent, run this calculation for your specific vehicle and rack.

Most factory crossbars carry a dynamic load rating of 100–150 lbs. That rating applies while driving. Rooftop tents require a static load rating — the weight the rack holds when parked and occupied.

Example using the Apollo A86 M at ~130 lbs:

- Tent: 130 lbs
- Two occupants at 175 lbs each: 350 lbs
- Bedding and gear: 20 lbs
- **Total static load: 500 lbs**

A factory crossbar rated at 150 lbs dynamic is not engineered for 500 lbs static. Installing an RTT on factory crossbars risks roof damage and rack failure. Both Meedo models require an aftermarket rack rated at a minimum of 165 lbs dynamic load to support the tent itself — but that number does not account for occupants. Your aftermarket rack's static load rating should clear 500 lbs for a two-person setup, or ~350 lbs for solo use with the Zeus.

Never mount a rooftop tent to factory crossbars or a glass panoramic roof.

## Final Recommendation

If you move camp every few days, are new to rooftop tents, or are working with a tighter budget, the Meedo Apollo A86 M is the practical choice. The 3–5 minute setup is a manageable trade-off at $1,100, and the 4-season fabric holds up for multi-day trips in real weather.

If you break camp daily, drive trails with overhead debris, or want the fastest possible shelter deployment in deteriorating conditions, the Meedo Zeus I M is worth the $200 premium and the extra 20 lbs. The 60-second setup compounds in value across high-frequency use.

In either case, verify your aftermarket rack's static load rating before purchase. Skipping that step is the most common and expensive mistake in the RTT buying process.
